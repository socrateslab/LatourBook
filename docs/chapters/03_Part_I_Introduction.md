# 第一部分：如何展开关于社会世界的争议

*页码：30–37*

与所有科学一样，社会学始于惊异。这种震动可能会以许多不同的方式被记录下来，但触发人们充满激情地去驯服社会这头野兽的，总是一种矛盾的显现：某种东西同时是不可见却又切身可感的、被视为理所当然却又令人惊讶的、平淡无奇却又具有令人困惑的微妙性的。“我们生活在看似牢固确立的群体之中，可它们为什么转型得如此迅速？”“我们被其他我们无法控制、且看起来足够平淡和世俗的行动元驱使着去做事。”“有某种不可见的东西压在所有人身上，它比钢还要坚固，却又如此令人难以置信地易变。”“存在着一些力量，它们与自然科学家所研究的力量惊人地相似，却又有种独特的不同。”“这种顽固抵抗与反常复杂性的令人费解的混合体，似乎对探究敞开了大门，但它却蔑视所有的探究。” 很难找到一个不被这些令人迷惑的陈述中的一个或多个所震撼的社会科学家。难道这些难题不正是我们求知欲的源泉吗？是什么推动着我们投入如此多的精力去解开它们？

然而，触发这些接踵而至的震撼的东西，与被设计用来解释它们的解决方案之间，距离正变得越来越远。我将在第一部分中论证，尽管社会学的洞察力是正确的，但由于对“社会”一词不断缩小的定义所建议的解决方案，在许多方面已经使这些洞察中原本具有生产力和科学性的成分变质了。这就是为什么我想重新审视这些接踵而至的每一个问题并对其进行剖析，以便我们能够更新我们对什么是“关联”的定义。

忠实于相对论原则，我没有像大多数社会学教科书通常所做的那样，将社会领域划分为已经作为社会界域成员的行动者、方法和领域的清单，而是按照关于这个宇宙是由什么构成的争议类型，来组织本书的第一部分。我认为，通过检验五个主要的“不确定性”，有可能在社会科学的主要直觉之上更进一步：[^16]

* 群体的性质：行动者被赋予身份的方式存在着许多相互矛盾的途径；


* 行动的性质：在每一段行动历程中，各种各样的代理似乎都会闯入并取代最初的目标；


* 对象的性质：参与到互动中的行动元类型似乎保持着完全开放的状态；


* 事实的性质：自然科学与社会其余部分之间的联系似乎是持续争端源泉；


* 以及，最后，关于在社会的科学这一标签下所做研究的类型，因为在何种精确意义上可以说社会科学是经验性的，这一点从未明确过。



使得行动者网络理论（ANT）看起来如此不可信的原因在于，在走向任何地方之前，这五个不确定性必须一个叠一个地堆积在一起，每一个新的不确定性都使前一个变得更加令人困惑，直到最后才重新获得某种常识——但那只是在终点。到目前为止，大多数ANT的使用者都很少有耐心去等待，我不能怪他们。[^17]

读者将在这里发现一套复杂的指令，以使位移（displacement）变得更加昂贵、更加痛苦。这样做的原因是我想要打破一种习惯——即一转到描述时，就突然加速将“社会”、“社会因素”和“社会解释”等概念联系起来的习惯。当社会社会学家说出“社会”、“权力”、“结构”和“语境”这些词时，他们往往会直接跳向前去连接广阔的生活和历史阵列，去动员巨大的力量，去从混乱的互动中探查出戏剧性的模式浮现，去在眼前的案例中到处看到更多已知类型的例子，去揭示幕后操纵提线的一些黑暗力量。并不是说他们错了，因为旧有的社会关系确实以某种方式被打包了起来，以至于似乎能为许多令人费解的主题提供现成的解释。但现在是时候更仔细地审视这样组装起来的聚合体类型，以及它们相互连接的方式了。

当你希望发现那些最近才冒出来的、尚未成为“社会”正统成员的、新的意想不到的行动者时，你必须旅行到其他地方，并且配备非常不同的装备。正如我们即将看到的，在“社会”一词的两种用法之间，其差异就像学习如何在现有的高速公路上开车，与第一次去勘察一块因违背许多当地社区意愿而规划修路的崎岖崎岖土地之间的差异一样大。[^18] 毫无疑问，ANT更喜欢缓慢地旅行，在小路上步行，并从自己的口袋里支付任何位移的全部成本。

这种节奏变化的理由在于，ANT并不采取一种看似合理的立场并预先施加某种秩序，而是声称在让行动者充分展开他们所沉浸的全部争议之后，能够更好地找到秩序。这就像我们在对行动者说：“我们不会试图管束你们，让你们符合我们的范畴；我们会让你们展开你们自己的世界，只有在稍后，我们才会要求你们解释你们是如何决定定居下来的。” 定义和订立社会秩序的任务应该留给行动者自己，而不是由分析者来承担。这就是为什么，为了重新获得某种秩序感，最好的解决方案是追踪争议本身之间的连接，而不是试图去决定如何解决任何给定的争议。[^19] 对秩序、严谨和模式的寻找绝非被放弃了。它只是被重新安置到了更深一步的抽象之中，从而允许行动者去展现他们自己不同的宇宙，无论它们看起来多么反直觉。[^20]

正是社会理论中这种提升了的抽象层次，使得ANT起初很难被把握。然而，这种转变类似于一个地图绘制者在试图将一条外国海岸线的形状记录在一张纸上时所做的事情。她可能会竭力使探险家们送来的各种报告符合某种现有的几何格式——海湾必须是圆形，海岬是三角形，大陆是正方形。但在注意到这些记录所造成的无可救药的混乱之后（其中没有一个完全落入预先确定的形状），她会热切地接受任何提议，用一个完全抽象的笛卡尔网格来取代对几何严谨性的追求。然后，她将使用这个空网格耐心记录海岸线本身，允许它以地质历史所造成的同样曲折的方式被绘制出来。尽管仅仅通过经度和纬度来记录每一个报告的点可能显得愚蠢，但坚持只保留符合预先确定的几何形状的数据会更加愚蠢。同样，ANT声称，通过找到一种方法来记录不稳定且移动的参考系之间的纽带，而不是试图保持一个参考系的稳定，有可能追踪到更牢固的关系并发现更具启示性的模式。社会不再仅仅是由“个人”、“文化”、“民族国家”“大致”构成的，就像非洲不“大致”是一个圆形，法国不“大致”是一个六角形，康沃尔不“大致”是一个三角形一样。这没有什么好惊讶的，因为每一个科学学科都是在设计能够适应眼前数据的恰当相对论方面的一种缓慢训练。为什么唯独社会学被禁止发明自己的路径，而被要求固守显而易见的东西？ 既然地质学家已经接受了冰冷坚硬的大陆板块自由漂浮在从深海裂谷中渗出的炽热熔融海床之上的观念，难道他们不是，可以这么说，站在了“更坚实的地基”上吗？ 同样，ANT声称，如果我们克制去中断泛滥的争议，我们将找到一种更具科学性的方式来构建社会世界。我们，也应该找到我们坚实的地基：在流沙上。与人们经常说的恰恰相反，相对论是一种在数据上漂浮的方法，而不是溺死在其中。

然而，一旦关联社会学家所要吞咽的不确定性范围开始展开，借自地图学或物理学的比喻很快就会失效。在某些极端情况下，行动者似乎拥有一种不可思议的能力，能够对社会学家为了开始工作而理所当然假定的一切事物提出异议。回过头来看，像物理学家那样放弃由以太提供的固定参考系，如果与我们为了让行动者自由展开其自身世界制造活动的完全不可通约性而必须放手的东西相比，显得是一件相当简单的事情。[^21] 准备好抛弃行动元、结构、心灵、时间以及空间，连同每一个其他哲学和人类学范畴，无论它们在常识中显得多么根深蒂固。

以我们的地图绘制者为例，这就像她不仅要处理来自许多旅行者的多份报告，还要处理多个投影网格，其中每一个点都要求其自身的特设坐标。面对这种混乱，人们可以决定限制争议的范围，或者释放所有的争议。第一种前相对论的解决方案运作良好，但冒着将社会学局限于常规、冰冷和安静境况的风险。第二种相对论的解决方案则应对活跃、温暖和极端的境况，但这样一来，人们就必须让争议一路展开。在两种立场之间达成某种妥协将是最荒谬的，因为争议不仅不是一个需要被拒之门外的麻烦，相反，它正是允许社会得以确立以及各种社会科学能够为它的构建做出贡献的东西。发展这些学科中的许多困难，都来自于拒绝变得足够理论化，以及来自于一种与对政治相关性的不合时宜的渴望混合在一起的、试图死守常识的错置尝试。这正是我希望尝试并尽可能长时间地维持的极端立场。缺点是在整个旅程中，读者不得不靠一种奇怪的饮食来支撑自己：他们必须以关于社会是由什么构成的争议为食。

我恐怕不得不说，与ANT一起旅行将被证明是痛苦地缓慢。移动将不断被五种类型的不确定性所中断、干扰、破坏和流离失所。在ANT试图穿越的世界中，如果没有昂贵且痛苦的翻译（translations），似乎没有任何位移是可能的。社会社会学家似乎像天使一样滑行，几乎无形地传送着权力和连接，而ANT学者则不得不像蚂蚁一样艰苦跋涉，携带着沉重的装备，以便产生哪怕是最微小的连接。在本书结束时，我们将尝试通过提出三个问题来总结区分一个好的ANT叙述与一个坏的叙述（一个至关重要的质量测试）的标准：旅程的所有困难都被承认了吗？从一个连接到下一个连接的完整旅行成本都完全支付了吗？旅行者没有通过偷偷搭乘已经存在的“社会秩序”的便车来作弊吧？ 在此期间，我的建议是尽可能少打包行李，别忘了买票，并为延误做好准备。

# Part I: How to Deploy Controversies About the Social World

_Source pages: 30–37_

Like all sciences, sociology begins in wonder. The commotion might be registered in many different ways but it’s always the paradoxical presence of something at once invisible yet tangible, taken for granted yet surprising, mundane but of baffling subtlety that triggers a passionate attempt to tame the wild beast of the social. ‘We live in groups that seem firmly entrenched, and yet how is it that they transform so rapidly?’ ‘We are made to do things by other agencies over which we have no control and that seem plain and mundane enough.’ ‘There is something invisible that weighs on all of us that is more solid than steel and yet so incredibly labile.’ ‘There exist forces that are strangely similar to those studied by natural scientists and yet distinctively different.’ ‘This puzzling mixture of obdurate resistance and perverse complexity seems wide opened to inquiry, and yet it defies all inquiries.’ It would be hard to find a social scientist not shaken by one or more of these bewildering statements. Are not these conundrums the source of our libido sciendi? What pushes us to devote so much energy into unraveling them?

There is, however, an increasing distance between what triggers those successive shocks and the solutions that have been devised to explain them. I am going to argue in Part I that although the insights of sociology are correct, the solutions suggested by a shrinking definition of the social has in many ways adulterated what was productive and scientific in them. This is why I want to reexamine each of those successive questions and dissect them so that we can renew our definition of what is an association.

Faithful to relativist principles, instead of dividing the social domain as most textbooks of sociology usually do into a list of actors, methods, and domains already taken as members of the social realm, I have organized the first part of this work by types of controversies about what this universe is made of. I think it is possible to build upon the major intuitions of the social sciences by examining five major uncertainties:[^16]

- the nature of groups: there exist many contradictory ways for actors to be given an identity;

- the nature of actions: in each course of action a great variety of agents seem to barge in and displace the original goals;

- the nature of objects: the type of agencies participating in interaction seems to remain wide open;

- the nature of facts: the links of natural sciences with the rest of society seems to be the source of continuous disputes;

- and, finally, about the type of studies done under the label of a science of the social as it is never clear in which precise sense social sciences can be said to be empirical.

What has made ANT so implausible is that before going anywhere those five uncertainties have to be piled on top of one another, with each new one making the former even more puzzling until some common sense is regained—but only at the end. Most users of ANT have so far had little patience to wait and I can’t blame them.[^17]

The reader will discover here a set of complicated instructions to make displacement more costly and more painful. The reason for this is that I want to break the habit of linking the notions of ‘society’, ‘social factor’, and ‘social explanation’ with a sudden acceleration in the description. When sociologists of the social pronounce the words ‘society’, ‘power’, ‘structure’, and ‘context’, they often jump straight ahead to connect vast arrays of life and history, to mobilize gigantic forces, to detect dramatic patterns emerging out of confusing interactions, to see everywhere in the cases at hand yet more examples of well-known types, to reveal behind the scenes some dark powers pulling the strings. Not that they are wrong since its perfectly true that older social relations have been packaged in such a way as to seem to provide a ready explanation for many puzzling subjects. But the time has come to have a much closer look at the type of aggregates thus assembled and at the ways they are connected to one another.

When you wish to discover the new unexpected actors that have more recently popped up and which are not yet bona fide members of ‘society’, you have to travel somewhere else and with very different kinds of gear. As we are going to see, there is as much difference in the two uses of the word ‘social’ as there is between learning how to drive on an already existing freeway and exploring for the first time the bumpy territory in which a road has been planned against the wishes of many local communities.[^18] There’s no question that ANT prefers to travel slowly, on small roads, on foot, and by paying the full cost of any displacement out of its own pocket.

The reason for this change of tempo is that, instead of taking a reasonable position and imposing some order beforehand, ANT claims to be able to find order much better after having let the actors deploy the full range of controversies in which they are immersed. It is as if we were saying to the actors: ‘We won’t try to discipline you, to make you fit into our categories; we will let you deploy your own worlds, and only later will we ask you to explain how you came about settling them.’ The task of defining and ordering the social should be left to the actors themselves, not taken up by the analyst. This is why, to regain some sense of order, the best solution is to trace connections between the controversies themselves rather than try to decide how to settle any given controversy.[^19] The search for order, rigor, and pattern is by no means abandoned. It is simply relocated one step further into abstraction so that actors are allowed to unfold their own differing cosmos, no matter how counter-intuitive they appear.[^20]

It is this increased level of abstraction in social theory which makes ANT hard to grasp at first. And yet this shift is comparable to what a cartographer does in trying to record the shape of a foreign coast on a piece of paper. She might exert herself to fit the various reports sent by explorers into some existing geometrical format—bays have to be circles, capes triangles, continents squares. But after noticing the hopeless mess created by those records, none of which exactly fall into pre-determined shapes, she will eagerly accept any proposition to displace the quest for geometrical rigor with a totally abstract Cartesian grid. Then she will use this empty grid to patiently record the coastline itself, allowing it to be drawn in as tortuous a way as geological history made it to be. Although it may appear stupid to record every reported point simply by longitude and latitude, it would be even more stupid to insist that only data that fits a preordained geometrical shape be kept. Similarly, ANT claims that it is possible to trace more sturdy relations and discover more revealing patterns by finding a way to register the links between unstable and shifting frames of reference rather than by trying to keep one frame stable. Society is no more ‘roughly’ made of ‘individuals’, of ‘cultures’, of ‘nation states’ than Africa is ‘roughly’ a circle, France a hexagon or Cornwall a triangle. There is nothing surprising in this since every scientific discipline is a slow training in devising the right sort of relativism that can be adapted to the data at hand. Why would sociology alone be forbidden to invent its own path and be requested to stick to the obvious? Now that geologists have accepted the notion of cold and rigid continental plates floating freely over the hot, molten seabed that seeps out of deep oceanic rifts, are they not, so to speak, on ‘firmer ground’? Similarly, ANT claims that we will find a much more scientific way of building the social world if we abstain from interrupting the flood of controversies. We, too, should find our firm ground: on shifting sands. Contrary to what is so often said, relativism is a way to float on data, not drown in them.

Metaphors borrowed from cartography or from physics break down very fast, however, once the range of uncertainties to be swallowed by sociologists of association begins to be deployed. In some extreme situations, actors seem to have an uncanny ability to disagree with everything sociologists supposedly take for granted in order to begin their work. Abandoning the fixed frame of reference offered by ether, as physicists did, appears in retrospect a rather simple affair when compared with what we will have to let go of if we want to leave the actors free to deploy the full incommensurability of their own worldmaking activities.[^21] Be prepared to cast off agency, structure, psyche, time, and space along with every other philosophical and anthropological category, no matter how deeply rooted in common sense they may appear to be.

Using the example of our cartographer, it is as if she had to deal not only with multiple reports coming from many travelers but also with multiple projection grids, where each point is requesting its own ad hoc coordinates. Faced with this confusion, one may decide to restrain the range of controversies or to unleash all of them. The first pre-relativist solution works fine but risks limiting sociology to routine, cold, and quiet situations. The second relativist solution tackles active, warm, and extreme situations, but then one has to let controversies unfold all the way. Striking some compromise between the two positions would be most absurd since controversies are not simply a nuisance to be kept at bay, but what allows the social to be established and the various social sciences to contribute in its building. Many of the difficulties in developing those disciplines have come from a refusal to be theoretical enough and from a misplaced attempt at clinging to common sense mixed with an ill-timed craving for political relevance. Such is the extreme position I wish to try and sustain for as long as possible. The drawback is that throughout their travels readers have to support themselves on a strange diet: they have to feed off controversies about what the social is made out of.

Traveling with ANT, I am afraid to say, will turn out to be agonizingly slow. Movements will be constantly interrupted, interfered with, disrupted, and dislocated by the five types of uncertainties. In the world ANT is trying to travel through, no displacement seems possible without costly and painful translations. Sociologists of the social seem to glide like angels, transporting power and connections almost immaterially, while the ANT-scholar has to trudge like an ant, carrying the heavy gear in order to generate even the tiniest connection. At the end of this book, we will attempt to summarize what differentiates a good ANT account from a bad one—a crucial quality test—by asking three questions: have all the difficulties of traveling been recognized? Has the complete cost of the travel from one connection to the next been fully paid? Has the traveler not cheated by surreptitiously getting a ride from an already existing ‘social order’? In the meantime, my advice is to pack as little as possible, don’t forget to pay your ticket, and prepare for delays.
